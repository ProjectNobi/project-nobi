Frequently Asked Questions

Honest answers to the hard questions about Nobi's model, sustainability, and mission.

🌍 Mission & Vision

What's the long-term vision? ▼

Digital companionship as accessible as Wikipedia, as resilient as Bitcoin, as personal as a diary.

An AI companion for every human that no company can take away, censor, or monetise against your interests. Free forever. Community-funded. Open source. Privacy-first.

Read the full vision at projectnobi.ai/vision.

How does Nobi benefit the Bittensor network? ▼

Most Bittensor subnets are infrastructure — they don't charge end users nor have a revenue path. So why do people still stake into them? They exist because the network funds them through emissions. That's how Bittensor works.

Nobi is the same — except we do something unique: we put Bittensor directly in front of millions of regular people.

That's the butterfly effect:

1. Person discovers Nori → just wants an AI companion
2. Nori works great → they tell friends, it spreads organically
3. Curious user asks "what powers this?" → discovers Bittensor
4. Some buy TAO → demand increases
5. Some become miners → network grows
6. Media covers "the free AI that no company owns" → cycle accelerates
7. Bittensor becomes a household name — not because of a whitepaper, but because of a product people use every day

No other subnet is building this path to the general public. Most subnets serve developers, researchers, or infrastructure. Nobi serves everyday humans.

Nobi is the Trojan horse that brings Bittensor to the masses. The subnet's value isn't measured in revenue — it's measured in how many people it introduces to the network.

That's worth staking on.

What's the risk? Could this fail? ▼

Yes, honestly. If the community doesn't stake, emissions stay low, miners leave, and quality drops. That's the real risk. It's the same risk every open-source project faces — will people care enough to support it?

We're betting they will, because privacy-preserving AI companionship is something people actually want and can't get from corporations. But we're transparent: this is a conviction bet, not a guaranteed outcome.

What we can control: building the best possible product. What the community controls: whether they believe in it enough to support it.

Isn't giving out value for free without any path to revenue just... bad economics? ▼

"Demand comes from value to the subnet" — correct! And what creates value? Users. Millions of them. Chatting every day. Creating demand for miner compute. Which creates demand for ALPHA stake. Which creates... wait for it... value to the subnet 🎉

Google gave away free search for 6 years before making a penny. Facebook was free for 8 years. Bittensor itself has been emitting TAO for years without "revenue." Were they all foolish, or were they just building something people actually want first?

Revenue follows usage. Usage follows a product people love. We're at step 1. We may never be directly "profitable" as a subnet — and that's the point. But we will bring millions of adopted users to Bittensor. And when those users discover what this network can do? Every subnet benefits. Every TAO holder benefits. Every miner benefits.

That's not bad economics. That's a Trojan horse 🐴

🤖 Product & Differentiation

How is Nobi different from ChatGPT or other AI assistants? ▼

Three structural differences — not just features:

Ownership: ChatGPT can delete your history, change policies, or shut down. Nobi memories are encrypted on a decentralised network — no single entity controls them.

Privacy: OpenAI may train on your conversations. Nobi memories are AES-128 encrypted, stored across independent miners. Your data, your control.

Persistence: ChatGPT doesn't truly know you across sessions. Nobi builds a persistent knowledge graph — your name, preferences, relationships, emotional patterns — that compounds over months and years.

The pitch isn't "better ChatGPT." It's: "Your AI companion that no company can take away from you."

What does "mass adoption for Bittensor" actually mean? ▼

Millions of regular people using Bittensor-powered AI without knowing it's Bittensor. Just like you use the internet without knowing about TCP/IP.

Today, roughly 50,000 people know what Bittensor is — all technical. Nobi's goal is 1 million+ regular people chatting with an AI companion on Telegram or Discord, getting a great experience, and never needing to know about subnets, TAO, or miners.

Their usage creates demand for the network. More users → more miners needed → more TAO staked → TAO becomes more valuable. Every person chatting with Nori is proof that Bittensor works at consumer scale.

Think of Android — billions use it, 99.99% have no idea it's Linux underneath. But their usage makes the entire Linux ecosystem more valuable.

Is there a market for this outside the Bittensor community? ▼

Absolutely. The market is everyone who wants an AI companion but doesn't trust corporations with their personal data:

• Privacy-conscious users (growing rapidly post-GDPR, post-Cambridge Analytica)
• People in countries where corporate AI is censored or unavailable
• Users burned by AI companies changing terms (Replika removing features, Character.AI restricting content)
• Anyone who wants genuine AI memory persistence without corporate lock-in

The Bittensor part is invisible to end users. They just see a bot that remembers them. The decentralisation is the how, not the what.

Is Nobi just a consumer app or an actual subnet? ▼

Both — and that's the point. Every subnet is infrastructure for something. SN1 is infrastructure for text generation. SN19 for image generation. Nobi is infrastructure for personal AI companionship.

The subnet provides the decentralised, censorship-resistant, privacy-preserving backbone with custom quality scoring. The bot and app are the user-facing interface. One doesn't work without the other.

💰 Sustainability & Funding

How can Nobi be free forever? Who pays for it? ▼

Nobi runs on the Bittensor network, which pays miners and validators through TAO token emissions — the same way every Bittensor subnet works. Miners earn TAO for serving quality companion responses. The network funds the infrastructure, not user subscriptions.

Think of it like public roads — you don't pay a toll every time you drive. The infrastructure is funded by a larger system so individuals can use it freely.

LLM inference costs have dropped 99% in two years and continue falling. Our miners currently run on $30-70/month VPS servers using API inference (Chutes at $20/month base + pay-as-you-go). As technology improves, costs go down while quality goes up.

Who pays the miners if users don't pay? ▼

Bittensor network emissions pay miners. Every Bittensor subnet receives TAO emissions proportional to its stake. Miners compete to serve the best companion responses — validators score them on quality, memory recall, personality consistency, and emotional intelligence. Better miners earn more TAO.

This is the standard Bittensor model. Miners aren't doing charity — they're earning TAO for providing a service, just like miners on any other subnet.

What does "burn 100% of owner emissions" mean? ▼

Every Bittensor subnet owner receives a mandatory 18% of emissions — this cannot be set to zero. Most subnet owners keep this as profit.

We burn 100% of ours. Using Bittensor's native burn_alpha() function, we destroy every token we receive. Every burn transaction is on-chain and publicly verifiable by anyone. No profit for the team. Ever.

This burn also benefits the community — it reduces token supply, creating deflationary pressure that helps all token holders.

How can the community support Nobi? ▼

Voluntary staking. TAO holders who believe in the mission can stake on the Nobi subnet. This increases emissions to miners and validators, which improves infrastructure quality. It's a virtuous cycle powered by community conviction.

Unlike a donation, staking is reversible — you can unstake at any time. You also earn ALPHA token emissions while staked. And because we burn 100% of owner emissions, the ALPHA supply decreases over time.

No one is required to stake. No one is pressured. The model works because people who want better AI companionship for the world choose to support it — like donating to Wikipedia or contributing to Linux.

Disclaimer: This is not financial advice. Staking TAO involves risk. Do your own research.

Shouldn't you charge a small fee at certain limits? ▼

That's the conventional wisdom. And it works — for companies that need to return investor money. We don't have investors. We have no equity to return. The 18% owner emissions get burned on-chain (burn_alpha()) — verifiable by anyone. There's literally no entity extracting profit.

The moment you add a paywall — even a small one — you've:

1. Created a barrier to adoption in developing countries (our target)
2. Required payment processing (GDPR, PCI compliance, entity formation)
3. Changed the incentive from "serve users well" to "convert free users to paid"
4. Lost the moral authority of "truly free" that differentiates us from every competitor

Wikipedia serves 1.7 billion monthly visitors on ~$170M/year in donations. They've been "unsustainable" for 23 years. Sometimes the right model is conviction-funded, not transaction-funded.

Isn't staking on Nobi just a bad investment with declining value? ▼

This misunderstands how dynamic TAO (dTAO) staking works. Stakers don't just lose value — they receive ALPHA tokens proportional to their stake. If the subnet has real usage and real utility, ALPHA demand increases, ALPHA price rises. Stakers can unstake at any time.

It's not a one-way donation — it's a market signal. Good subnets attract stake because their ALPHA is worth holding. Bad ones lose stake and die. That IS the mechanism for positive return: useful subnet → more users → more stake inflow → ALPHA appreciation.

And the 100% burn of owner emissions via burn_alpha() actually helps stakers — it reduces ALPHA supply, creating deflationary pressure on the token. Every burn makes existing ALPHA more scarce.

Disclaimer: This is not financial advice. Staking involves risk. Do your own research.

Why not just ask for donations instead? ▼

Donations don't create an incentive loop. Staking does. When someone stakes on Nobi:

• Miners get paid → better infrastructure → better product
• ALPHA supply decreases (our burns) → ALPHA value benefits
• Staker earns ALPHA emissions → they're compensated for their support

A donation is gone forever. Staking is reversible, earns yield, and creates network effects. It's structurally superior to donations for sustainability.

Subscriptions are proven — aren't you misunderstanding consumer behaviour? ▼

Subscriptions work. But they're not the only model. Counter-examples:

• Wikipedia: 1.7B monthly visitors, ~$170M/year in voluntary donations, 23 years running
• Signal: 100M+ users, funded by Signal Foundation, no subscription
• Linux: Runs 90%+ of cloud infrastructure, community-funded
• Firefox: Community-funded browser serving hundreds of millions

The pattern: products that serve a public good can sustain on conviction funding IF the mission resonates. The question is whether "AI companion that no company can take away" resonates enough. We're betting it does.

If it's free and good, won't people exploit it? ▼

Free doesn't mean unlimited. Multiple layers of protection:

Rate limiting. Configurable limits per user — currently 300 messages/day, 10 per minute. Casual users never hit these. Abusers do. Limits adjust per development phase.

Quality-based scoring. Miners are scored on response QUALITY, not volume. There's no incentive to serve spam or abuse — only quality earns TAO.

Falling costs. LLM inference costs drop roughly 10x every 18 months. We're building on a cost curve that works in our favour.

At massive scale — yes, it requires significant miner infrastructure funded by significant stake. We're not pretending it's free to operate. We adapt at each stage: limits tighten, models adjust, efficiency improves.

The product is free. Abuse is not.

If Nobi doesn't charge users, where does the money come from? ▼

Miners pay for their own servers. Bittensor pays the miners. Users pay nothing.

Think of Uber — Uber doesn't buy cars for drivers. Drivers buy their own cars because driving is profitable. Same model: miners buy their own servers because mining is profitable. They earn TAO for serving quality companion responses. If it's not profitable, they leave. If the subnet grows, more join.

What the project does NOT pay for:

• Miners fund their own servers ($30-70/month each, varies by phase)
• Validators fund their own servers
• User inference at scale: handled by miners

This is a community and open-source project. The founder team bootstraps the rest — all self-funded, no VC, no grants, no token sales — and will continue to do so. Operational sustainability at mainnet will be discussed when we reach that milestone.

We believe if our product provides real value to massive end-users, everything will be paid off.

Why not build on existing subnets instead of creating a new one? ▼

We considered this. The problem: Nobi needs a custom incentive mechanism. Our validators score miners on memory recall quality, personality consistency, emotional intelligence, and multi-turn conversation — not raw inference speed or storage capacity.

Existing infrastructure subnets (Targon, Chutes, Hippius) optimise for different things. None of them measure "is this a good AI companion?" That requires a dedicated subnet with custom validation logic.

We do use Chutes for inference as a fallback — the subnets complement each other. But the Nobi subnet IS the mechanism that makes miners compete specifically on companion quality.

🔒 Privacy & Security

How is my data protected? ▼

Your memories are encrypted with AES-128 encryption before being stored. They're distributed across independent miners on the Bittensor network — no single company owns or controls your data.

You have full control: use /memories to see what Nori knows, /export to download everything, /forget to delete it all permanently. Your data, your choice.

Can miners read my conversations? ▼

Miners store encrypted memory blobs — the content is encrypted before it reaches them. However, this is server-side encryption, not end-to-end encryption. During processing, miners do see conversation content to generate responses.

On-device privacy (where raw data never leaves your device) is on our roadmap for mainnet — it's not yet live. We're honest about current limitations while working toward stronger privacy guarantees.
